Wetlands Delineation

A contractor looking to build a spec home outside of Powderhorn was looking into combining two land parcels into a single unit and placing the home in the center. Before this could happen however an assessor would have to come out to the property to evaluate the status of any potential wetlands at the builders expense.

I used Google Earth Engine to conduct a Supervised Classification on the most current NAIP imagery. Using the National Wetlands Inventory I was able to train my samples for this classification on relevant wetland areas that had already undergone a current onsite assessment. In addition, and informed by prior remote wetland evaluations of the area from the 1980s, I pulled the Near-Infrared Imagery as well. Then I took the liberty of blending the two outputs in a way which allowed me not only to accurately locate the wetlands on the property but classify them as well.

Backcountry Hazards

Communicating backcountry avalanche hazards to recreational users who have a more limited understanding of terrain and snowpack complexities is one of the primary responsibilities of avalanche forcasters. One of my personal favorite models used to accomplish this task is Avalanche Terrain Exposure Scale (ATES).

Traditional models for rating avalanche danger are based on the stability of snow, which changes regularly with the weather â€“ from day to day, or even hour to hour. Terrain however, doesn't change much. The angle and shape of the ground, or the number of established avalanche paths won't vary from day to day. By using the ATES, you can begin to measure your skills, experience and risk tolerance against the landscapes through which you choose to travel.

In this map I was able to isolate terrain features by slope and aspect to highlight only the slopes considered steep enough to slide. Some of which are prone to wind-loading, cornice formation, and generally pose a greater threat (Red), and others that while not as sensitive to the same weather related events are still steep enough to slide (Purple).

Urbanization

How do we measure urbanization and change in regions where comprehensive data is limited? This was the challenge I was facing in my work with the board of tourism in Bahia Ballena where illustrating the rapid change the area has been experiencing would play an important role in the adoption of an urban development plan aimed at sustainable growth.

Using high resolution 4-band satellite imagery along with a 25m digital elevation model. Zonal statistics were run on the Normalized Vegetation Change Index for the watershed sub-catchments within the district. This was used to identify the areas undergoing the greatest change in relationship to their downstream impact on a protected coral reef. From here a study area was established and RGB composite imagery was used to construct a model highlighting the new structure growth against those that existed prior to 2016.

US. Drought Monitor

The US Drought Monitor is an exceptionally complete dataset. The challenge in working with it is that 52 weeks of data drawing in on top of one another looks like a mess. In order to abstract it into something usable that communicates a clear message a number of things had to happen. This map is a Bivariate Tessellation of all the data collected in 2022 which was then stylized after the WPA maps of the 1930's.

This map in particular was a joy to create. It not only gave me the opportunity to work with a new set of analytical tools and introduced me to python coding, but it left me a lot of room for stylistic design choices and freedom in how I could present the message the data was communicating.
